# Frontier Exploration: Personal Experimentation Results

> Capturing what didn't work for YOU — distinct from general anti-patterns

## The Gap

The Seed covers:
- **Negative knowledge**: Anti-patterns, failure modes, lessons learned (general)
- **Trade-off knowledge**: Context-dependent decisions
- **Personal knowledge**: Insights, decisions, experiences

But there's a gap: **Personal experimentation results** — specific things YOU tried that didn't work for YOUR specific context, situation, or preferences.

## Why It Matters

1. **Personal validity**: Something that works for others may not work for you. Capturing this helps avoid repeating failed experiments.

2. **Context preservation**: Your specific situation (equipment, time, preferences) affects what works. General advice can't capture this.

3. **Time savings**: Future-you doesn't remember that you already tried X and it failed.

4. **Honest knowledge base**: A KB that only shows successes is misleading. Your failures are valid data points.

## What Makes This Different From Negative Knowledge

| Negative Knowledge | Personal Experimentation Results |
|-------------------|--------------------------------|
| Universal: doesn't work for anyone | Personal: didn't work for YOU |
| General principle | Specific instance |
| Can be cited from sources | Only you have this data |
| Example: "Searing doesn't seal in juices" | Example: "I tried meal-prepping on Sundays and hated it" |

## When to Capture

Capture personal experimentation results when:
- You tried something specifically (not just "read about it")
- You can articulate WHY it didn't work for you
- The failure is specific to your context, not universal
- You'll likely consider trying again in the future

## Structure

```yaml
---
experiment: What you tried
context: Your specific situation (time, equipment, preferences)
outcome: What happened
reason-for-failure: Why you think it didn't work
retry-conditions: When you might try again
---
```

## Example Note

### My Failed Experiment: Sunday Meal Prep

**What I tried:** Batch cooking all meals for the week on Sunday.

**Context:** 
- Working from home
- Prefer variety in meals
- Have 2 hours available on weekends

**What happened:** By Wednesday, I was eating food I didn't want because I was "tired" of it. Food quality degraded.

**Why it didn't work for me:** I need variety. Eating the same thing 5 days in a row makes me not want to eat.

**Would I try again?** Only if I could do 3-day batches with different meals.

## Test

Can you answer: "What have you tried that didn't work for you, and why?" If not, you may have a gap in personal experimentation capture.
