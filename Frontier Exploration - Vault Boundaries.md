# Frontier Exploration: Vault Boundaries — What Belongs in a Knowledge Base

> "Not everything you know belongs in your second brain. But how do you decide?"

## The Problem

The Seed provides extensive guidance on HOW to structure notes, but offers no guidance on WHAT should enter the vault in the first place. This creates ambiguity:

- Should I save that random thought about tomorrow's meeting?
- What about project files, code snippets, or raw data?
- Where do I put daily journal entries?
- What about notes I'll only need once?

Every vault eventually faces boundary creep — accumulating notes that aren't really "knowledge" but rather "operational debris." Without explicit boundaries, the vault becomes a dumping ground.

## The Core Tension

There's a fundamental tension between two valid impulses:

1. **Inclusivity**: Capture everything "just in case" — you never know what might be useful
2. **Curation**: Only keep what truly enhances your thinking — the vault is a garden, not a landfill

The Seed's current philosophy leans toward curation ("process within 48 hours") but doesn't explicitly define the boundary.

## Dimensions of Belonging

Consider these questions when deciding if something belongs in the vault:

### 1. Reusability
- **Keep if:** You'll need this knowledge in multiple contexts over time
- **Exclude if:** You'll use it once and never reference again
- **Gray area:** Things you need now but might need later

### 2. Synthesizability
- **Keep if:** Connecting this to other notes creates insight
- **Exclude if:** This is a standalone fact with no connections possible
- **Gray area:** Things that COULD connect but don't yet

### 3. Durability
- **Keep if:** This knowledge remains true/useful for months or years
- **Exclude if:** This becomes obsolete within days/weeks
- **Gray area:** Time-sensitive knowledge that has a defined useful lifespan

### 4. Transferability
- **Keep if:** You'd share this with a colleague working on the same problem
- **Exclude if:** This is purely personal/ephemeral (private diary, daily log)
- **Gray area:** Things you'd share but only with close collaborators

## Proposed Boundary Categories

### Core Vault (Always Keep)
- Atomic knowledge notes
- Concept definitions and explanations
- Procedural knowledge (how-to, tutorials)
- Source references and provenance
- Hub notes and navigation structure

### Operational Vault (Debatable)
- Project-specific notes that expire when project ends
- Meeting notes that are only relevant for a short window
- Task lists and action items
- Draft documents that aren't yet "knowledge"

### Personal Vault (Separate)
- Daily journals and diaries
- Personal reflections not meant for reuse
- Private information not related to knowledge synthesis

### Ephemeral (Excluded)
- Temporary bookmarks
- One-off calculations or scratch work
- Information you'll use once and discard
- Data that belongs in a database, not a knowledge base

## The "Future Self" Test

A practical heuristic: Will my future self thank me for having this note, or curse me for cluttering the vault?

**Thank you for:**
- Synthesized insights that took time to develop
- Connections between ideas that weren't obvious
- Sources that verify important claims
- Procedures that save time when repeated

**Curse you for:**
- Thousands of unprocessed bookmarks
- Notes that say "look at this URL"
- Duplicate information scattered across notes
- Notes you haven't read/updated in a year

## Edge Cases

### "I'll process this later"
The 48-hour rule exists for a reason. But what about notes that are genuinely "capture now, decide later"? A potential solution: have a separate "inbox" that MUST be processed within 48 hours or deleted.

### Domain-Specific Ephemeral Data
In some domains, data that's normally ephemeral becomes valuable:
- Stock prices on specific dates (historical analysis)
- Weather patterns over time (climate knowledge)
- Version numbers and changelogs (software evolution)

The rule: if ephemeral data ACCUMULATES into patterns, it becomes knowledge.

### The "One Note" Exception
Sometimes a single note defies categorization but should still be kept:
- A chance insight that hasn't connected yet
- A seed of an idea that will grow
- A note that feels important but you can't explain why

Trust intuition here. The vault can hold a few mysteries.

## Proposed Seed Rule

**Rule:** Define explicit vault boundaries — categorize what you keep (core knowledge), what you tolerate (operational), and what you exclude (ephemeral).
**Why:** Without boundaries, the vault accumulates noise, search quality degrades, and the "second brain" becomes a dumping ground. Explicit boundaries enable intentional curation.
**Test:** Can you list: (1) What types of notes are always kept? (2) What types are reviewed and either integrated or deleted? (3) What types are never added? If you can't answer these, the boundary is undefined.

**Rule:** Use a staging area (inbox) for uncategorized captures — process within 48 hours or explicitly defer with a review date.
**Why:** Capture first, process second is good advice, but "process" must have a deadline. Unprocessed captures become permanent clutter.
**Test:** Does an inbox exist? Are there notes older than 48 hours that aren't marked for later review?

**Rule:** Separate operational knowledge from synthesized knowledge — project notes, meeting notes, and task lists should either be time-boxed (auto-deprecate) or clearly marked as temporary.
**Why:** Operational debris looks like knowledge but isn't. It clutters searches and misleads about the vault's actual contents.
**Test:** Pick 10 random notes. Can you categorize each as: (a) permanent knowledge, (b) time-boxed operational, or (c) should be deleted?
