AI-Assisted Knowledge Management Seed

Drop this file into any new vault’s CLAUDE.md to bootstrap a healthy, self-improving knowledge base. Every rule below has been tested and proven by a live, AI-maintained knowledge vault.

1. Best Practices Conformance

Rules that ensure any new knowledge base starts healthy.

Rule: Maintain human ownership over AI-assisted knowledge work. Why: Delegating thinking to AI degrades the cognitive benefits of knowledge management. Test: Check if you can articulate the key insights from notes you created with AI help without looking at them.

Rule: Process matters more than output — writing is thinking. Why: The act of writing consolidates understanding; outsourcing it loses the learning. Test: After AI drafts a note, do you rewrite it in your own words? If not, you’re skipping the thinking.

Rule: Verify all AI suggestions, don’t trust them. Why: AI can hallucinate facts, links, and relationships that don’t exist. Test: Check 3 AI-suggested links — can you explain WHY each connection exists in one sentence?

Rule: Start with a _root.md note defining the vault’s purpose. Why: Without a clear purpose, the knowledge base lacks direction and coherence. Test: Can you state your vault’s purpose in one sentence? Does every note connect back to this purpose?

Rule: Add 3 foundational notes before capturing content — Atomic Note, Linking, Graph Traversal. Why: Foundation notes establish conventions early; retrofitting is harder than starting right. Test: Do new notes reference these foundations? Are they linked in new notes’ frontmatter?

Rule: Process documents into atomic notes before saving — capture ≠ save. Why: Unprocessed bookmarks and copied text don’t become knowledge; only processed notes are discoverable. Test: Count your unprocessed bookmarks. If > 10, you’re hoarding not building.

Rule: Use diverse ingestion mechanisms — direct input, document processing, media capture, system integration. Why: Multiple channels ensure comprehensive knowledge capture; single points of failure lose insights. Test: Can knowledge enter your vault through multiple channels (manual, API, webhook, scraping)?

Rule: Process incoming knowledge within 48 hours — timely processing preserves quality. Why: Unprocessed content degrades knowledge base quality over time. Test: When was your oldest unprocessed capture? Is it < 48 hours old?

Rule: Know what NOT to capture — skip easily retrievable, ephemeral, duplicate info. Why: Not everything deserves a note; selective capture keeps the vault valuable. Test: Do you ask “will I need this in 6 months?” before creating notes?

Rule: Knowledge compounds better than money — synergistic returns, no capital needed, non-rivalrous. Why: Notes combine to create insights greater than the sum; sharing multiplies value. Test: Can you point to an insight that emerged from combining two notes?

Rule: Build a knowledge legacy — document clearly, share openly, connect broadly. Why: Knowledge outlasts the creator; notes help future learners. Test: Could someone understand your notes without you? Would they find them valuable?

Rule: Diversify your knowledge portfolio — core (30%), domain (40%), adjacent (20%), exploratory (10%). Why: Diversification creates resilience and cross-domain innovation. Test: Can you categorize your notes into these proportions? Are you balanced?

Rule: Your second brain shapes your knowledge identity — you become someone who connects ideas naturally. Why: The vault changes how you think; thinking in networks becomes automatic. Test: Do you naturally see connections between ideas? Has your thinking style changed?

Rule: Your second brain multiplies, not adds — one note connects to many, each connection = insight potential. Why: Notes compound in value; multiplication beats addition. Test: Can you trace how one note connects to multiple insights?

Rule: Create a CLAUDE.md file in vault root with vault purpose, structure, and agent guidelines. Why: AI agents need explicit instructions to navigate and maintain the vault correctly. Test: Does your CLAUDE.md exist? Does it define the vault’s purpose and agent behavior?

Rule: Externalize, network, augment — the extended mind shift from brain-only to brain+network. Why: Biological limits constrain memory; networked tools extend recall, association, and intelligence beyond skull limitations. Test: Have you shifted from memorizing to organizing? Do you think of your vault as an extension of your mind?

Rule: Build a learning stack with 5 integrated layers: capture, processing, storage, review, output. Why: Individual tools don’t create systems thinking; integrated layers ensure knowledge flows from input to output. Test: Do you have all 5 layers? Can knowledge flow from capture through to output?

Rule: Use note templates for consistency — atomic note, MOC, project templates. Why: Templates ensure all needed fields are captured and speed up note creation. Test: Do you use templates? Can you create a new note from a template in <1 minute?

Rule: Use distinct note types — atomic, MOC, structure, project, reference, archive. Why: Different purposes need different formats; mixing types creates confusion. Test: Can you categorize your notes by type? Do you use the right template for each?

Rule: Choose note-taking method based on purpose — Zettelkasten for research, PARA for projects, Cornell for studying. Why: Different methods suit different needs; forcing one size fits all reduces effectiveness. Test: Have you chosen methods aligned with your goals? Do different contexts use different approaches?

Rule: Use Progressive Summarization for capture, then convert to atomic Zettelkasten notes for linking. Why: PS provides fast compression; Zettelkasten provides connection and emergence; combining them gets both benefits. Test: Do you use both methods? Can you trace knowledge from capture through to linked atomic notes?

Rule: Treat your vault as a knowledge operating system — it manages resources, runs applications, enables functionality. Why: The OS metaphor captures how second brain organizes resources (knowledge), runs processes (thinking), enables action (decision-making). Test: Can you articulate what your knowledge OS does? Does it manage resources, enable thinking, power decisions?

Rule: AI agents should make one atomic change per commit. Why: Large, scattered commits are hard to review and revert; atomic changes are clear and manageable. Test: Can you describe each commit in one sentence? Does each commit do exactly one thing?

Rule: Write descriptive commit messages that describe the knowledge added/changed. Why: Vague messages (“iteration 2”) don’t help trace the vault’s evolution. Test: Can you understand what changed by reading commit messages? Are they informative?

Rule: Ignore cache files — never commit .mcp-cache/, .obsidian/, *.log. Why: Generated files bloat the repo and cause merge conflicts.

Rule: Use tags to complement links, not replace them — links are stronger connections. Why: Tags are loose associations; links create navigable relationships. Test: Can you reach every tagged note via links? Are tags supplemental?

Rule: Use templates for common note types — ensures consistency and complete information capture. Why: Templates speed up creation and ensure nothing is missed; they capture all needed fields. Test: Do you have templates? Can you create a new note from a template in <1 minute?

Rule: Capture quickly, process within 24-48 hours — ideas are fleeting. Why: Ideas escape if not captured fast; processing later loses context and momentum. Test: Do you have a quick capture method? Can you capture an idea in <2 minutes?

Rule: Don’t over-tag — 2-5 tags per note is plenty. Why: Excessive tags create noise and dilute meaning. Test: Count tags per note. Is each tag meaningful? Remove generic tags. Test: Check your .gitignore — are cache directories excluded?

Rule: Start simple — write notes and link them before creating elaborate templates or folders. Why: Complexity before content leads to system abandonment; simple first, refine later. Test: Can you write a note and link it without consulting any templates? If not, you’re over-organizing.

Rule: Track note metadata (last-reviewed, review-count) in frontmatter. Why: Review history enables spaced repetition and identifies neglected notes. Test: Do your notes have last-reviewed dates? Can you find stale notes easily?

Rule: Use Zettelkasten principles — atomic notes, unique IDs, meaningful linking. Why: The method is proven (Luhmann wrote 70+ books from his Zettelkasten); it turns the vault into a thinking partner. Test: Are your notes atomic? Do they have unique identifiers? Can the graph generate insights through connections?

Rule: Use active retrieval, not passive re-reading — close your notes and try to recall. Why: The effort of retrieving strengthens memory; passive reading/highlighting doesn’t create learning. Test: When reviewing, do you close notes and try to recall? Or just re-read?

2. Best Practices Catalog

The testable list of what “healthy” means.

Rule: Engage actively with notes — think before reading. Why: Passive reading produces no new knowledge; engagement is where connections form. Test: Before reading a note, ask: “What questions does this raise? What connects to what I already know?”

Rule: Capture thoughts immediately — don’t trust memory. Why: Ideas fade; writing them down preserves the insight and enables future retrieval. Test: When a thought arises while reading, do you create an atomic note right away? Or plan to “remember it later”?

Rule: Write to think, not just to record — use the Feynman Technique. Why: Writing clarifies ideas and reveals gaps in understanding; passive transcription doesn’t create knowledge. Test: Can you explain a concept from your notes simply? Does writing reveal insights you’d miss just reading?

Rule: Each thought becomes one atomic note, linked to its origin. Why: Scattered thoughts in long notes are hard to find; atomic notes are discoverable and linkable. Test: Check your recent notes — can each be understood in isolation? Does it link back to where it originated?

Rule: Only keep what you process into atomic notes — don’t save everything. Why: Note hoarding creates noise; unprocessed bookmarks and copied text don’t become knowledge. Test: Count your unprocessed bookmarks. If > 10, you’re hoarding not building.

Rule: Write notes that are self-contained — future you should understand them without clicking links. Why: Notes that depend on other sources become meaningless when links break or sources disappear. Test: Can you understand each note without clicking any links? Can you verify each claim from memory?

Rule: Always cite sources and write notes independently of any single source. Why: Single-source dependency means notes don’t make sense without the original; you can’t verify claims. Test: Can you understand your note without clicking any links? Can you verify each claim from memory?

Rule: Write notes in your own words — encoding in your own language strengthens retention. Why: Copy-pasting doesn’t create understanding; rewriting consolidates knowledge. Test: Can you explain a note’s concepts without looking at it? Did you write it in your own words?

Rule: Manage note lifecycle — seedling → bud → evergreen → archive. Why: Notes evolve; understanding lifecycle prevents over-attachment to outdated notes. Test: Can you categorize notes by lifecycle stage? Do you archive notes that are no longer relevant?

Rule: Follow insertion strategy — search first, analyze neighbors, create atomic, link appropriately. Why: Prevents duplicates and ensures new knowledge integrates into the existing graph. Test: Before adding a note, do you search for existing related nodes? Can you explain where it fits?

Rule: Track metrics — node count, edge density, hub distribution, isolated nodes. Why: Quantitative metrics reveal health; without tracking, problems go unnoticed. Test: Do you track node count and edge density? Can you identify imbalances?

Rule: Use graph visualization for exploration, not just pretty pictures — find gaps and clusters. Why: Visualization reveals structure that text hides; use it to find problems, not decorate. Test: When did you last use graph view to find gaps? Can you identify underserved areas?

Rule: Use hybrid mind — brain for creativity/judgment, second brain for storage/retrieval. Why: Each excels at different tasks; over-relying on either reduces effectiveness. Test: Do you balance brain work (thinking) with external work (storage)?

Rule: Use atomic notes as first principles — combine to form reasoning chains and new insights. Why: Atomic notes are fundamental concepts that can be freely combined; this enables first-principles thinking. Test: Can you trace a solution back to atomic fundamentals? Do combinations create new insights?

Rule: Follow the knowledge pipeline: input → capture → process → connect → output. Why: Structured flow ensures knowledge doesn’t get stuck; output creates feedback for more input. Test: Can you trace knowledge from input through to output? Is there a flow?

Rule: Practice interleaving — mix topics and create cross-domain connections deliberately. Why: Cross-domain insights emerge from unexpected connections; single-domain silos miss these opportunities. Test: Do you link notes across different domains? Can you find analogies between fields?

Rule: Treat your vault as a personal knowledge API — consistent input, processing, output. Why: Like software APIs, reliable systems produce reliable results; consistency builds trust in the system. Test: Can you predict what insights will emerge? Is your knowledge system reliable?

Rule: Use graph over hierarchy — link by association, not location. Why: Graphs enable discovery, serendipity, and emergence; hierarchies constrain and duplicate. Test: Do you organize by links first? Can you find notes by connection, not folder?

Rule: Embrace network effects — value increases with graph density, not note count. Why: Graphs improve with scale; folders worsen; the more connections, the more insights emerge. Test: Is your graph density increasing? Do unexpected connections create new insights?

Rule: Take ownership — curate your own inputs, process actively, build your own structure. Why: Passive learning produces passive knowledge; ownership drives deeper understanding. Test: Do you curate your own learning? Do you add your own interpretation to notes?

Rule: Turn consumption into creation — process, add insights, share. Why: Creation deepens learning and builds reputation; passive consumption alone has limited value. Test: Do you create from what you consume? Can you point to something you created from your notes?

Rule: Invest in the meta-skill — learning how to learn compounds across all domains. Why: This skill underlies everything else; returns are infinite. Test: Do you treat knowledge management as a foundational skill? Does it improve everything else?

Rule: Focus on systems, not goals — daily processes over binary outcomes. Why: Systems are continuous and actionable; goals focus on outcomes and discourage when missed. Test: Do you have daily/weekly systems? Can you measure process rather than just outcomes?

Rule: Build legacy — document clearly, share openly, connect broadly. Why: Knowledge outlives us; good notes help others learn and build on your work. Test: Could someone understand your notes without you? Would they find them useful?

Rule: Knowledge compounding beats money compounding — no capital needed, synergistic returns, non-rivalrous. Why: Notes connect to create insights greater than sum; sharing multiplies value. Test: Can you point to an insight that emerged from combining notes? Do connections exceed notes?

Rule: Use MCP graph tools — graph_search, graph_hubs, graph_related, graph_isolated_nodes. Why: AI navigation requires graph tools; they enable discovery that manual navigation misses. Test: Can you use graph tools to find connections? Do you use them for navigation?

Rule: Use descriptive commit messages — describe the knowledge added, not “iteration N”. Why: Vague messages don’t help trace the vault’s evolution. Test: Can you understand what changed by reading commit messages?

Rule: Build self-referential accuracy — notes must follow their own rules. Why: A knowledge base about knowledge management should exemplify its own principles. Test: Do your notes follow the rules they prescribe? Can you verify self-conformance?

Rule: Skip easily retrievable info — don’t note what web search finds in 5 seconds. Why: Not all info deserves a note; capturing noise dilutes signal. Test: Can you find this info quickly online? If yes, skip the note.

Rule: Diversify knowledge portfolio — core (30%), domain (40%), adjacent (20%), exploratory (10%). Why: Diversification creates resilience and enables innovation from cross-pollination. Test: Is your knowledge diversified? Do you have both depth and breadth?

Rule: Track provenance — know the source of each piece of knowledge. Why: Provenance enables verification and helps understand knowledge lineage. Test: Can you trace each note back to its source? Do you know where knowledge originated?

Rule: Handle outdated information — date everything, use deprecation markers, don’t delete. Why: Notes become outdated; without handling, the vault becomes unreliable. Test: Do you have frontmatter with dates? Do you deprecate outdated notes instead of deleting?

Rule: Trust but verify — don’t trust notes blindly, check sources and freshness. Why: Notes may contain outdated info, misremembered facts, or incomplete understanding. Test: Do you verify notes before using them? Can you point to recent verifications?

Rule: Use atomic notes as first principles — combine freely to rebuild understanding. Why: Atomic notes are fundamental concepts; links form reasoning chains; combinations create new principles. Test: Can you trace a complex idea back to atomic fundamentals? Do combinations create new insights?

Rule: Keep notes atomic — one idea per note, 200-300 word max. Why: Atomic notes are reusable, linkable, and enable efficient graph traversal; large notes become unwieldy and hard to connect. Test: Can you summarize this note in one sentence? Does it contain exactly one concept? If it’s > 300 words, split it.

Rule: Review notes using spaced repetition — increasing intervals (1→3→7→14→30 days). Why: The forgetting curve causes decay; periodic review just before forgetting consolidates long-term memory. Test: Do you have a review schedule? Can you point to notes reviewed at increasing intervals?

Rule: Links provide retrieval cues — more connections = more recall paths. Why: When you see a related note, it triggers recall; the graph becomes a memory augmentation system. Test: Do you use links as memory triggers? Can you trace multiple paths to key concepts?

Rule: Use tools to serve knowledge, not the reverse — don’t let tool selection become procrastination. Why: Obsessing over the perfect app means no knowledge gets captured; the best tool is the one you actually use. Test: Have you switched note-taking apps in the last 3 months? If yes, how much knowledge was lost in the transition?

Rule: Adapt the second brain principles to your domain — structure is universal, content is specific. Why: Universal principles (atomic notes, linking, graph traversal) apply to any field; customize hub nodes and templates for your domain. Test: Have you identified domain-specific hub nodes? Can you adapt templates for your field?

Rule: Do weekly reviews — Monday inbox, Wednesday graph health, Friday knowledge review. Why: Weekly hits the sweet spot (daily too frequent, monthly too infrequent); maintains vault health. Test: Do you have a weekly review habit? Can you summarize last week’s work in one sentence?

Rule: Adapt the second brain to any domain — universal principles (atomic, linking, graph) apply everywhere; only content differs. Why: The methodology is domain-agnostic; structure remains, content adapts. Test: Have you identified hub nodes for your domain? Do universal principles apply to your specialty?

3. Node Refinement

How to improve existing notes without breaking them.

Rule: Write new notes from your thoughts, don’t just annotate existing ones. Why: Annotating blends thoughts with source material; new notes create searchable, linkable assets. Test: Count your original insights this month vs. annotated existing notes. Are you creating, or just commenting?

Rule: Write for your future self, not for perfection — iterate later. Why: Perfectionism leads to no notes being published; iteration improves notes over time. Test: Have you revised any note more than 3 times without publishing? Ship it and improve later.

Rule: Review 3 random notes daily — update, link, or confirm they’re fresh. Why: Regular review catches outdated info, finds linking opportunities, and reinforces knowledge. Test: Do you have a daily review habit? Can you point to 3 notes reviewed this week?

Rule: Iterate on notes rather than abandoning them — evolve from seedling to evergreen. Why: Abandoned notes become dead ends; evolved notes compound in value over time. Test: Can you trace a note’s evolution? Has any note grown from seedling to evergreen?

Rule: Only log significant improvements in iteration logs — not every small change. Why: Logging every commit creates noise; significant milestones are what matter. Test: Is your iteration log concise? Does it show meaningful progress, not micro-commits?

Rule: Never delete notes — only deprecate them. Why: Deleted notes break incoming links; deprecation preserves the graph while marking content as outdated. Test: Have you ever permanently deleted a note? Did it break links? Deprecate instead.

4. Structure Development

How the knowledge base structure should evolve over time.

Rule: Let structure emerge from links, not from folders. Why: Hierarchical folders constrain discovery; graph connections reveal unexpected relationships. Test: Do you spend more time organizing folders than linking notes? Stop — links create structure.

Rule: Use PARA for folders (function), Zettelkasten for links (knowledge) — they complement each other. Why: PARA provides action orientation; Zettelkasten provides knowledge connection; together they cover both productivity and learning. Test: Do your folders map to Projects/Areas/Resources/Archives? Do Resources/ contain linked knowledge notes?

Rule: Create cross-domain notes — combine insights from different fields. Why: Emergence happens at intersections; notes from single domains can’t create novel insights. Test: Do you have notes that link concepts from different domains? Search for cross-domain connections.

Rule: Use hub nodes as entry points for broad topics — every major theme needs a hub. Why: Hubs provide navigation anchors; without them, the graph is hard to explore. Test: Run graph_hubs — are your top hubs actually important topics? Or just frequently linked filler?

Rule: Create hub nodes when a topic has 5+ related notes without a central entry point. Why: Hubs provide navigation shortcuts and reduce depth to any node; without them, exploration is linear. Test: Run graph_hubs — are your top hubs covering your main topics? Do any topics have 5+ notes but no hub?

Rule: Search by connection, not location — use graph_search, graph_related, graph_hubs. Why: Unlike folders, graphs let you find information by relationship, not by where it’s stored. Test: When looking for something, do you search by connection first? Or by folder/date?

Rule: Review hub nodes monthly — add connections, split if too broad. Why: Hubs can become bloated over time; regular review keeps them focused and useful.

Rule: Maximum 3 hops from any hub to reach any node. Why: Beyond 3 hops, relevance drops rapidly; deep chains are hard to traverse and indicate structure problems. Test: Can you reach any note from a hub in ≤3 clicks? If not, create intermediate hubs. Test: When did you last review your top 5 hubs? Are any becoming unwieldy?

5. Interconnection Integrity

How to verify that links are correct and valuable.

Rule: Link quality over quantity — every link must have context. Why: Link inflation creates a false sense of connectivity; meaningless links hurt more than help. Test: Can you explain WHY each note links to another in one sentence? If not, remove the link.

Rule: Every note should link to 2-3 related notes minimum. Why: Orphan notes are invisible in the graph; connections enable discovery and emergence. Test: Run graph_isolated_nodes — are any notes truly unlinked? Connect or archive them.

Rule: Create connections through 5 types: direct relevance, contrast/comparison, analogy, cause/effect, category. Why: Different connection types create different insights; variety strengthens the network. Test: Can you categorize your links? Do you have all 5 types represented?

Rule: Run graph health checks weekly — check isolated nodes, link density, node sizes. Why: Neglected vaults accumulate problems; proactive maintenance catches issues early. Test: When did you last run graph health checks? Is there a weekly maintenance habit?

Rule: Capture quickly, process later — use an inbox but don’t try to fully process at capture time. Why: Perfect processing at capture time slows down idea capture; separate capture from processing. Test: Do you have an inbox? Can you add quick notes without fully elaborating them?

Rule: Every link must answer “Why should the reader follow this?” — no decorative links. Why: Random keyword matches and “also see” links dilute meaning and waste reader time. Test: Can you explain in one sentence why each link exists? If not, remove it.

Rule: Create multiple paths to important information — redundancy aids retrieval. Why: Single points of failure mean information becomes unreachable when links break. Test: Can you reach key notes through multiple routes? Are important concepts linked from multiple notes?

6. Knowledge Enrichment

How to integrate new knowledge into existing notes.

Rule: Use progressive summarization — summarize content in multiple passes, not all at once. Why: First pass is fast (bold key points), later passes deepen only when needed; this balances depth with time. Test: Do you capture first, then progressively distill? Or try to write perfect notes in one go?

Rule: Progressive summarization process: capture → bold notable points → convert to bullets → write in your own words → link to network. Why: Each pass serves a purpose: capture (preserve), distill (identify), structure (organize), synthesize (encode), connect (network). Test: Can you trace each piece of knowledge through these stages?

7. Note Size Management

Detecting and splitting oversized notes.

Rule: Split notes that exceed 300 words or contain multiple ideas. Why: Large notes become unwieldy, hard to link, and lose the benefits of atomicity; splitting keeps each note focused and discoverable. Test: Is the note > 300 words? Does it contain multiple distinct concepts? If yes to either, split it into atomic notes and rewire links.

Rule: Rebuild graph index after bulk additions or major restructuring. Why: Stale indexes miss new connections and produce incorrect search results. Test: Did you rebuild the index after the last bulk import? Does graph_search return expected results?

Rule: When splitting: identify distinct ideas, create new atomic nodes, preserve core in original, link bidirectionally, update all affected links. Why: Splitting without rewiring breaks the graph; bidirectional links maintain navigability. Test: After a split, can you reach both notes from multiple paths? Are all incoming links rewired?

8. Knowledge Integration

Combining existing notes with new ideas.

Rule: Share synthesized insights, not raw captures — process before sharing. Why: Raw inbox items lack context; sharing unprocessed notes confuses others and damages credibility. Test: Before sharing, can you answer: atomic? linked? sourced? readable?

9. Continuous Exploration

Anti-stagnation rules that keep the system developing.

Rule: Use AI for exploration, but keep humans in the loop for decisions. Why: AI can scale exploration but lacks judgment about what matters to you. Test: Review your last 5 AI-generated actions — did a human approve each one before committing?

Rule: Use principles over tools — don’t let tool selection become procrastination. Why: Obsessing over the perfect app means no knowledge gets captured; the best tool is the one you actually use. Test: Have you switched note-taking apps in the last 3 months? If yes, how much knowledge was lost in the transition?

Rule: Run regular audits — don’t only add, never review. Why: Neglected vaults accumulate broken links, outdated info, and dead ends; maintenance keeps health. Test: When did you last run a health check? If > 30 days ago, schedule an audit.

Rule: Follow growth targets — start small (10 nodes), scale gradually (25→50→100). Why: Rapid growth without foundation leads to a messy vault; gradual growth builds habits. Test: Are you hitting weekly node targets? Does the vault feel manageable at current size?

Rule: Configure MCP servers (vault-graph, vault-sources) for AI navigation. Why: AI agents need graph tools to discover connections and navigate the knowledge base effectively. Test: Are MCP servers configured? Can AI agents use graph_search and graph_hubs?

Rule: Consistency matters — don’t skip days. Why: Compound effects depend on regular contribution; sporadic bursts underperform consistent small gains. Test: How often do you add notes? Is there a regular cadence (daily/weekly)?

Rule: Trust the compound — effects take time to emerge. Why: Early notes feel isolated, but network effects kick in around 50-100 notes; don’t abandon before the tipping point. Test: Are you past 50 notes? Do you see organic connections forming?

Rule: Daily review (10 min) beats weekly review (1 hour) — consistency compounds. Why: Daily engagement maintains freshness and creates more connections than sporadic intense sessions. Test: Do you review daily? Is there a 10-minute routine?

Rule: Don’t just add — synthesize. Create notes from intersections. Why: Emergence requires combining existing notes; passive accumulation doesn’t create new insights. Test: When did you last create a note from combining two existing notes? Can you point to it?

Rule: Run a feedback loop — REFLECT → backlog → execute → metrics → REFLECT. Why: Without structured iteration, vaults stagnate; the loop ensures continuous improvement. Test: Is there a regular cycle? Does the vault improve itself over time?

Rule: Refill backlog before it empties — never run out of tasks. Why: Empty backlog leads to aimless sessions; pre-filled backlog ensures productive heartbeats. Test: Is the backlog always populated? Are there at least 5 items ready?

Rule: Push through the startup phase — early notes feel useless but momentum builds. Why: The knowledge flywheel requires initial effort; once spinning, insights emerge with less effort. Test: Have you passed the tipping point where adding notes triggers multiple connections automatically?

Rule: Use graph_hubs to find entry points, graph_related for discovery. Why: Hubs reveal the vault’s core concepts; 2-hop exploration reveals unexpected connections. Test: Can you name your top 5 hubs? Have you used graph_related to find hidden connections?

Rule: Build a curiosity loop: capture → connect → discover → more curiosity. Why: Each turn amplifies the next; the vault becomes a self-reinforcing learning engine. Test: Does adding a note make you want to add more? Does the graph reveal unexpected insights?

Rule: Think in decades, not days — don’t optimize for today’s results. Why: Knowledge compounds over years; short-term stress about structure misses the long-term value. Test: Can you imagine your vault in 10 years? Are you building for the long game?

Rule: Innovation comes from combining ideas — new+new, existing+existing, or new+existing. Why: The vault’s value is in making combinations possible; diverse knowledge enables more innovations. Test: Have you created a note from combining two existing notes? Can you point to it?

Rule: Knowledge compounding beats money compounding — no capital required, synergistic returns (A+B > A+B), non-rivalrous sharing. Why: Unlike money, knowledge has no startup cost, creates emergence, and multiplies when shared. Test: Have you experienced knowledge synergy? Has combining notes created insights neither had alone?
