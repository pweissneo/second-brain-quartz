# Frontier Exploration: Sensory Knowledge Capture

> How do you capture knowledge about taste, smell, texture, and other sensory experiences in a knowledge base?

## The Problem

Some knowledge domains are fundamentally sensory:
- **Cooking** — taste, texture, aroma, mouthfeel
- **Wine/coffee** — flavor profiles, aroma complexity
- **Perfumery** — scent notes, longevity, sillage
- **Music** — timbre, tone quality
- **Textiles** — hand (feel), drape, texture

The Seed's tacit knowledge rules acknowledge that some knowledge resists articulation. But sensory knowledge has a specific structure: it's **comparative**, **subjective**, and **experiential**.

## Why Current Rules Are Incomplete

The Seed has:
- Subjective knowledge tagging (claim-type: subjective, consensus markers)
- Tacit knowledge capture (failure modes, proxies)
- Multi-modal knowledge (media separation)

But it lacks guidance for:
1. **Sensory reference systems** — How do you create a common vocabulary?
2. **Comparative sensory frameworks** — Describing "this is like X but more Y"
3. **Intensity scaling** — How to express "very salty" vs "slightly salty" consistently
4. **Modal cross-references** — "Sounds like a middle C played on a Stradivarius" (cross-modal metaphors)

## The Challenge

Sensory knowledge is:
- **Inherently comparative** — "It tastes like burnt caramel" only works if you know burnt caramel
- **Highly subjective** — "Spicy" means different heat levels to different people  
- **Embodied** — You must experience it to understand it
- **Cultural** — Flavor preferences are learned, not innate

## Proposed Rules

### Sensory Reference Standards

**Rule:** Create explicit sensory reference standards — documented examples that serve as benchmarks.
**Why:** Without reference standards, sensory descriptions are meaningless to readers who haven't had the experience.
**Test:** Can you point to 3 reference standards in your sensory domain? (e.g., "ripe banana" as a sweetness reference, "00 steel wool" as a texture reference)

### Cross-Modal Anchors

**Rule:** Use cross-modal anchors when available — connect sensory experiences to more universal references.
**Why:** "It smells like cut grass" is more accessible than "it has green notes" for those unfamiliar with wine jargon.
**Test:** Can a beginner understand the sensory description without domain expertise?

### Intensity Scales

**Rule:** Define explicit intensity scales for your domain — document what "mild", "medium", "strong" actually mean.
**Why:** Subjective qualifiers vary wildly between people. Explicit scales enable consistent communication.
**Test:** Can two different people use your scale and arrive at similar assessments?

### Sensory Profiles Over Single Descriptors

**Rule:** Capture complete sensory profiles, not single descriptors — note aroma, flavor, texture, finish.
**Why:** Single descriptors ("fruity") are meaningless in isolation. Profiles create meaningful comparison.
**Test:** Does each sensory note include 3+ dimensions of description?

## Domains Where This Matters Most

- Culinary arts
- Wine and beverage tasting
- Coffee cupping
- Perfumery
- Cheese and specialty foods
- Music (timbre, tone)
- Textiles and materials
